Packing Non-Self-crossing Edge-disjoint Spanning Paths into a Point Set

The term packing refers to the arrangement of multiple geometrical structures or shapes such as circles, squares, triangles, or polygons into a fixed and finite set of points.

Packing strategies help to construct and arrange multiple geometric structures in a predetermined bounded space; hence, it can be classified as an optimization problem, as we are trying to allocate the optimal space for resources in a finite bounded space. The better the efficiency of the algorithm, the greater number of items that can be packed.

In our research, we have studied the packing of non-self-crossing, edge-disjoint plane spanning paths and have obtained some promising results.

Problem Statement

In our research, the topic of interest is packing spanning paths into any given point set P. The spanning paths to be packed should be non-self-crossing and edge-disjoint.

A path is called non-self-crossing if no two edges of the path cross. Two paths are edge-disjoint if they do not share an edge. Our goal is to pack two non-self-crossing and edge-disjoint spanning paths into P, such that all the edges of each path cross some line in the plane.

New Idea

Packing multiple geometric structures can raise some design considerations such as a concept called a blocked configuration. This blocked state prevents us from appyling any algorithm to acheive the desired output.

Hence, we introduce the new idea of Tangent Switch. We introduce an approach to exchange vertices incident on separate convex hulls of two different point sets, which in turn resolves the blocked configuration.

We implement the tangent switch rotating a common separating tangent until a pair of vertices between 2 point sets are exchanged.

Final Result

Once we resolve the blocked configuration with the help of the tangent-switch algorithm, the rest of the procedure is simply repeating the tangent-switch unless all blocked vertices are exchanged.

Thus, with my solution we demonstrated an alternate approach to pack 2 non-self-crossing and edge-disjoint spanning paths into a point set.

With more research it might be even possible to embed another path into the point set.

Retrospective

An open question still exists: Is it possible to extend the number of non-self-crossing edge-disjoint spanning paths that can be packed in a point set consisting of points in general position in the plane to three or more? Furthermore, instead of having multiple cases for each variation of a blocked configuration, a much more generic approach to the problem might also exist.
